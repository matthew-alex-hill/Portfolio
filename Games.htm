I Have No Mouth and You Must Hold
A Game About Learning to Speak.
The game simulates a device called the Voder developed by Homer Dudley in 1939. Here is a video showing the device in action. The controls of the game involve producing 2 synthesized tones and filtering them to produce vocalised noises like the voder.  There are 10 filter keys at the top of the keyboard (q-p) , space is used to toggle between the tones, and control toggles playback.  
The 10 filter keys activate a band pass filter ranging from &nbsp;0-255Hz and 5400-7500Hz (the values used in the original voder). This game is set in a call centre and gains its enjoyability from the difficulty placed into what would normally be a very mundane task, reading out a piece of information. This concept has worked well in titles like QWOP and Octodad, where the player must complete day to day tasks like walking or moving around objects with very in depth and difficult to master controls. However, the application of this concept to speech has never been done and there are very few games which explore speech and the intricacies of something we take so for granted. The player will be placed at a desk in front of a monitor which states which filters to apply for each vocal sound in a visual key. Their screen will also contain a script on a notepad which will contain a flow-chart of what they need to say depending on the user's reply. Below you can see a mock-up of what the game would look like. This concept art uses soft pastel like matte textures and dull colours like beige and black as well as the stock images and template design of the tutorial info to create an unsettlingly boring dystopian corporate atmosphere.
FM Autochess
A Football Manager mod which adds an autochess mode.
The genre of auto chess is one that has an intriguing and satisfying core loop in creating a team whose individual attributes feed into one another and synergise. However, I couldn't get into the genre through its most popular games as they relied on auto battler combat systems which I found too complex. However, the genre doesn't have to necessarily be applied to a battle system and may work even better in a sports tactics game as these games often revolve around assembling teams that play well as a collective anyway. I originally planned this game as a standalone football auto chess game but struggled to design the AI, which makes or breaks a game with automatic gameplay. I then realised that the auto chess mechanics could be built on top of another game with a match engine and the AI would be done for me. Football Manager is the obvious choice as it already contains many different player types and roles, so all that must be done to create a unit is give them attributes and a role and the game will make them play a certain way. I can then make the attributes increase when a character is levelled up and make classes which increase attributes of every other unit of that class, the more units of that class you have. Here is a mock-up of the team assembling UI.
This game mode may also be a unique and welcome experience to Football Manager players as it allows them to develop a tactic from a random list of players that they are gradually given without having to spend time on scouting, recruitment, training and fitness. This mode can distil the gameplay down to just developing a system around the players you get and adding players that fit the system.
